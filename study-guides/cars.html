Section 4 — CARS

Critical Analysis & Reasoning Skills

53 questions · 90 minutes · 9 passages, ~5–7 questions each · No science knowledge needed

⚠ CARS is a skill, not a knowledge subject
You cannot memorize your way to a high CARS score. You must practice reading and analyzing complex passages daily — at minimum 6 weeks of consistent practice. The guide below gives you strategy; execution comes from repetition.
CARS on Test Day

The actual CARS section consists of 9 passages with 5–7 questions each. Most questions ask you to interpret the author's argument, infer meaning, or evaluate the logic of an idea.

  • Use the passage itself as your authority — outside knowledge is rarely needed.
  • Track the main point and the author's attitude through each passage.
  • Spend about 10–11 minutes per passage, with extra time to review flagged items.

1. Section Overview

Passage Types

TypeFrequencyCharacteristics
Humanities~50%Philosophy, ethics, art, music, literature, history. Often argue a viewpoint or analyze a concept. Dense, nuanced arguments.

What CARS Measures

  • Ability to comprehend complex arguments
  • Identify the author's main idea and central thesis
  • Distinguish evidence from conclusions
  • Evaluate the strength of arguments
  • Make logical inferences beyond the text
  • Identify assumptions underlying arguments

2. Question Types

The 3 Main CARS Question Categories

Foundations of Comprehension (~30%)

Directly test understanding of what the author said.

Sub-typeWhat it asksStrategy
Main Idea / Central thesisWhat is the passage primarily about?Consider the whole passage; avoid too narrow or too broad answers
Detail / Fact retrievalWhat does the author say about X?Go back to the text; don't rely on memory
Meaning / Vocabulary in contextWhat does the word/phrase mean in context?Read surrounding sentences; consider author's argument
Tone / AttitudeHow does the author feel about X?Look for charged language, qualifiers, rhetorical devices

Reasoning Within the Text (~30%)

Test understanding of how the argument works.

Sub-typeWhat it asksStrategy
Evidence / ConclusionWhich statement best supports/challenges the author's argument?Identify the claim being supported; match evidence type
Logical structureWhy does the author include this paragraph/example?Think about how it serves the overall argument
Strengthen / WeakenWhich choice would most strengthen/weaken the argument?Identify the argument's hidden assumption first

Reasoning Beyond the Text (~40%)

Test ability to apply, extend, or evaluate the author's ideas.

Sub-typeWhat it asksStrategy
Application / New situationWhich scenario would the author most likely agree with?Stay consistent with author's logic, not your opinion
Evaluate the argumentWhat assumption does the author make? What is a flaw?Find gaps between evidence and conclusion
Extrapolation / ImplicationIf the argument is true, what would follow?Only accept conclusions that are logically entailed

3. Reading Strategies

The Golden Rule of CARS
Every answer must be supported by evidence in the passage. Your background knowledge and personal opinion are irrelevant — and often dangerous.

The MAPS Method

  • M — Main idea: What is the central thesis? What is the author arguing or explaining?
  • A — Argument structure: How does each paragraph contribute? Evidence vs. conclusion?
  • P — Purpose: Why did the author write this? Persuade? Analyze? Describe?
  • S — Scope: What is included? What is excluded? What are the limits of the argument?

Active Reading Approach

  1. Read the first paragraph carefully — It usually contains the thesis/central question
  2. For subsequent paragraphs: Identify the main point and its relationship to the thesis. Ask: "Does this support, qualify, contrast, or elaborate on the main argument?"
  3. Track the author's stance: Notice hedge words (may, might, perhaps, often) vs. strong assertions. Notice when author quotes others vs. their own view.
  4. Identify key structural words: However, Nevertheless, Although → contrast/concession. Therefore, Thus, Hence → conclusion. Furthermore, Moreover → addition. For example → evidence.
  5. Summarize each paragraph in 4–7 words as you go (mental or written)

Annotating Effectively

  • Mark the thesis (usually end of 1st paragraph)
  • Mark transitions between views
  • Mark evidence vs. conclusions
  • Note author's tone (skeptical, enthusiastic, cautious)
  • Mark anything that seems surprising or counterintuitive

Answering Questions

  1. Read the question stem carefully — identify what TYPE of question it is
  2. Predict the answer before looking at choices
  3. Eliminate wrong choices using the text
  4. Be wary of: extreme language (always, never, must), distortions of the author's view, and choices that require outside knowledge
  5. Between two choices: return to text; ask "which is better supported by the passage?"

4. Common Traps to Avoid

❌ Wrong Answer Types

  • Too extreme: Uses "always," "never," "all," "only"
  • Too narrow: Focuses on a detail, misses the main point
  • Too broad: Goes beyond what the passage actually claims
  • Opposite: Directly contradicts the author's view
  • Out of scope: True, but the author never discussed it
  • Distortion: Close to what author said, but subtly twisted
  • Requires outside knowledge: Based on information not in passage
  • Your opinion: What YOU believe, not what the author argues

✅ Signs of Correct Answers

  • Directly supported by the passage
  • Uses hedged language consistent with author's certainty
  • Consistent with the author's overall tone and stance
  • Scope matches: not too broad, not too narrow
  • For inference questions: the minimum required by the text
  • For "author agrees" questions: author would recognize as their view
  • For strengthen/weaken: addresses the specific claim/assumption
  • For application: author's logic applied consistently

Specific Pitfalls

  • Trap: "I already knew this was true" → The MCAT may present false information. Your job is to reason from WHAT THE PASSAGE SAYS, not from outside knowledge.
  • Trap: Misidentifying the author's view vs. views being criticized → Authors often describe opposing viewpoints before countering them. Make sure you know whose view you're attributing.
  • Trap: Missing the scope → The author may argue X is true in context Y; applying it to context Z is out of scope.
  • Trap: Absolute thinking → MCAT favors nuanced, hedged language. Absolute answers ("proves," "all," "only") are usually wrong.

5. Timing & Pacing

The Math

90 minutes ÷ 9 passages = 10 minutes per passage Each passage: ~3.5 min reading + ~6.5 min questions Per question: ~1 min average (some take 30 sec, some take 2 min)

Time Management Rules

  • Keep moving: If stuck on a question >90 seconds, flag and move on
  • Check time every 3 passages: You should be at ~30 min after passage 3, ~60 min after passage 6
  • Never skip a passage: Every passage question is worth the same; skip any WITHIN a passage, not the passage itself
  • Harder passages first or last? Personal preference — but know that passages about unfamiliar topics are often easier because you're forced to use only the text
  • Flag and return: Mark uncertain answers; come back if time allows. Always have an answer selected before moving on.

Passage Difficulty

If you find a passage particularly difficult (dense philosophical text, unfamiliar concepts), don't panic. These are often worth the same number of questions as easier passages. Strategy: read more carefully, focus on finding the main argument, and use process of elimination aggressively.

6. Practice Framework

Daily Practice Schedule

Weeks Before TestDaily PracticeFocus
10–8 weeks1 CARS passage/dayActive reading; passage mapping; don't time yourself yet
7–5 weeks2 CARS passages/dayTimed (10 min/passage); review explanations carefully
4–3 weeks3–4 passages/day (or full sections)Full 53-question sections; identify question type patterns
2–1 weeksFull CARS sectionsSimulate test conditions exactly; review wrong answers thoroughly

After Each Practice Passage

  1. Record: # correct, time taken, question types missed most
  2. For each wrong answer: identify WHY you got it wrong (trap type?)
  3. For correct answers you were uncertain about: understand why it's right
  4. Re-read the passage once more and confirm your main idea summary was correct
  5. Note any vocabulary or rhetorical techniques you didn't understand

Reading Outside MCAT

Read challenging nonfiction daily to build reading stamina and vocabulary:

  • Recommended: The New Yorker, The Atlantic, Harper's Magazine, Aeon, Plato/Aristotle, psychology/sociology textbooks
  • Practice identifying: Author's main claim, evidence vs. assertion, assumptions, tone
  • Aim for: Articles that challenge your existing views or are in unfamiliar fields
