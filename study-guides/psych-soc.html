What the MCAT really asks in P/S

P/S combines passage-based reasoning with study design, statistics, and sociocultural context. Many questions ask you to predict behavior, identify experimental variables, or explain population-level effects.

  • Practice experiment-based passages and data interpretation.
  • Remember that social determinants of health are a major MCAT theme.
  • Focus on how psychology and sociology relate to health, identity, and healthcare access.

Nervous System Divisions

  • Sympathetic ("fight or flight"): Preganglionic NT = ACh; Postganglionic NT = NE. ↑HR, ↑BP, dilates pupils, relaxes gut, ↑ blood to muscles
  • Parasympathetic ("rest and digest"): Both pre- and postganglionic = ACh. ↓HR, constricts pupils, stimulates digestion, erection (SLUDD: salivation, lacrimation, urination, defecation, digestion)
  • Somatic: Voluntary skeletal muscle; NT = ACh at neuromuscular junction

2. Sensation & Perception

Key Principles

  • Absolute threshold: Minimum stimulus intensity detected 50% of the time
  • Difference threshold (JND): Minimum difference between stimuli detected 50% of the time
  • Weber's Law: JND is a constant proportion of the original stimulus. ΔI/I = k (Weber fraction)
  • Signal Detection Theory: Separates sensitivity (d') from response bias (β); accounts for hits, misses, false alarms, correct rejections
  • Sensory adaptation: Decreased sensitivity to constant stimulation (not pain)
  • Sensory habituation: Decreased attention/response (more cognitive than sensory)

Vision

Light enters → cornea → lens (accommodation) → vitreous humor → retina. Rods: scotopic vision (dim light), peripheral, no color. Cones: photopic vision (bright light), central (fovea), color (S/M/L wavelengths). Optic nerve → lateral geniculate nucleus (thalamus) → primary visual cortex (occipital).

Gestalt Principles

The brain organizes visual information into wholes: Figure-Ground · Proximity (near = grouped) · Similarity · Continuity · Closure (complete incomplete figures) · Common fate (moving together = grouped).

Depth Perception Cues

Binocular: Retinal disparity (main cue), convergence (eyes turn inward for close objects).

Monocular: Relative size, interposition (overlap), linear perspective, texture gradient, motion parallax, aerial perspective (haze = distant).

Reinforcement Schedules

  • Continuous: Reinforce every time; fast acquisition, fast extinction
  • Fixed ratio: After every N responses (e.g., piecework pay); high, steady rate with post-reinforcement pause
  • Variable ratio: After unpredictable number (e.g., slot machine); highest, most resistant to extinction
  • Fixed interval: First response after fixed time rewarded (e.g., weekly paycheck); scallop pattern
  • Variable interval: First response after unpredictable time; steady, moderate rate

Other Learning Types

  • Observational learning (Bandura): Learning by watching others; Bobo doll experiment; modeling
  • Latent learning (Tolman): Learning without reinforcement; demonstrated later when needed; cognitive map
  • Habituation: Decreased response to repeated non-harmful stimulus
  • Sensitization: Increased response to repeated stimulus (after harmful experience)
  • Insight learning (Köhler): Sudden understanding; "aha moment"; chimps with banana

4. Cognition, Motivation & Emotion

Cognitive Biases & Heuristics

  • Availability heuristic: Judge probability by how easily examples come to mind (overestimate plane crashes)
  • Representativeness heuristic: Classify based on how similar to prototype (ignore base rates)
  • Anchoring: Over-rely on first information received
  • Confirmation bias: Seek/remember info that confirms existing beliefs
  • Framing effect: Same info presented differently influences decisions
  • Functional fixedness: Can't see alternative uses for objects

Motivation Theories

  • Maslow's Hierarchy: Physiological → Safety → Love/Belonging → Esteem → Self-actualization (pyramid; lower needs first)
  • Drive reduction theory (Hull): Behavior motivated by reducing internal tension
  • Incentive theory: Behavior pulled toward external rewards
  • Arousal theory: Optimal arousal level (Yerkes-Dodson: inverted U between arousal and performance)
  • Self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan): Intrinsic (internal reward) vs. extrinsic (external reward); intrinsic = more sustainable

Emotion Theories

  • James-Lange: Physical arousal → emotional interpretation (we feel afraid because we're trembling)
  • Cannon-Bard: Physiological and emotional responses occur simultaneously
  • Schachter-Singer (Two-factor): Arousal + cognitive label = emotion; misattribution of arousal possible
  • Facial feedback hypothesis: Facial expressions feedback to influence emotions

Stress

General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye): Alarm reaction (fight-or-flight) → Resistance (coping) → Exhaustion (if prolonged).

HPA axis: Stressor → Hypothalamus → CRH → Anterior pituitary → ACTH → Adrenal cortex → Cortisol. Chronic stress → immune suppression, hypertension, hippocampal damage.

Freudian Defense Mechanisms

  • Repression: Unconscious blocking of unacceptable thoughts
  • Regression: Return to earlier developmental behaviors under stress
  • Projection: Attribute own unacceptable feelings to others
  • Rationalization: Justify with logical-sounding reasons
  • Displacement: Redirect emotions to safer target
  • Sublimation: Channel unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable activities (most mature)
  • Reaction formation: Act opposite to actual feelings
  • Denial: Refuse to acknowledge reality

8. Social Psychology

Attribution Theory

  • Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE): Overattribute others' behavior to dispositional (internal) factors; underestimate situational factors. "He failed because he's lazy" (not "the exam was hard")
  • Actor-Observer Bias: Attribute our own behavior to situations, others' to dispositions
  • Self-serving bias: Attribute successes to internal, failures to external factors
  • Just world hypothesis: Believe people get what they deserve

Social Influence

  • Conformity (Asch): Change behavior/beliefs to match group norms. Factors: group size (3+), unanimity, cohesion, public behavior
  • Obedience (Milgram): 65% of participants delivered maximum "shock" when authority figure present. Factors: proximity of authority, prestige, diffusion of responsibility
  • Groupthink: Group pressure for conformity suppresses dissent; poor decisions
  • Social facilitation: Presence of others improves performance on easy tasks; impairs on difficult tasks
  • Social loafing: Reduced effort when in a group; more common in collectivist cultures
  • Deindividuation: Loss of self-awareness in crowds → increased antisocial behavior

Attitudes & Persuasion

  • Cognitive dissonance: Discomfort from inconsistent beliefs/actions → attitude change to restore consistency
  • Central route processing: Careful consideration of arguments; leads to lasting attitude change
  • Peripheral route processing: Superficial cues (attractiveness, number of arguments); less durable change
  • Foot-in-the-door: Small request first → large request later (commitment principle)
  • Door-in-the-face: Large request first (refused) → smaller request seems reasonable

Prejudice, Stereotyping & Discrimination

  • Prejudice: Affective attitude (feeling) toward a group
  • Stereotype: Cognitive belief about group characteristics (not necessarily negative)
  • Discrimination: Behavioral treatment based on group membership
  • Implicit bias: Unconscious stereotypes; measured by IAT
  • In-group/out-group bias: Favor own group; view out-group as homogeneous
  • Contact hypothesis (Allport): Equal-status contact between groups reduces prejudice

Social Stratification

  • Social class: Based on income, wealth, education, occupation, prestige
  • Social mobility: Vertical (up/down) vs. Horizontal (same level). Meritocracy vs. ascriptive factors.
  • Intersectionality (Crenshaw): Multiple identities (race, class, gender) create overlapping systems of discrimination
  • Health disparities: SES strongly predicts health outcomes; social determinants of health: income, education, housing, neighborhood, access to care

Culture & Socialization

  • Culture: Shared beliefs, values, norms, symbols, language. Material vs. Non-material culture.
  • Socialization: Primary (family) → Secondary (school, peers, media)
  • Social norms: Folkways (minor customs), Mores (moral norms), Laws (formalized norms), Taboos (strongest)
  • Deviance: Behavior violating norms. Labeling theory (Becker): being labeled deviant → deviant identity. Strain theory (Merton): anomie when can't achieve cultural goals through legitimate means.

Demographic Concepts

  • Demographic transition model: Societies move from high birth + death rates → low birth + death rates as they industrialize
  • Incidence: New cases in a period / population at risk. Prevalence: All existing cases / total population at a given time
  • Epidemiology: Study of disease distribution and determinants. Relative risk, odds ratio.

Goffman's Dramaturgical Model

Social life = theatrical performance. Impression management: We present different "faces" in different situations. Front stage (public performances) vs. Backstage (private self). Stigma: Deeply discrediting attribute that reduces someone from a whole person to a tainted, discounted one.
